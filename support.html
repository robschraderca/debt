Know Your Rights

Your Rights Under the FDCPA

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) is a federal law that protects you from abusive, unfair, and deceptive debt collection practices. Here's what you need to know.

Limited Contact Hours

Collectors cannot contact you before 8 a.m. or after 9 p.m. in your local time zone. They also cannot contact you at work if you tell them your employer disapproves.

Right to Stop Contact

You can send a written request to stop all communication. After receiving it, the collector can only contact you to confirm they'll stop or to notify you of specific actions.

Debt Validation

Within 5 days of first contact, collectors must send you a written notice with the amount owed, creditor name, and your right to dispute. You have 30 days to request validation.

No Harassment or Abuse

Collectors cannot use threats of violence, obscene language, repeatedly call to annoy you, or publish your name as someone who doesn't pay debts.

No False Statements

Collectors cannot lie about the amount you owe, falsely claim to be attorneys or government representatives, or threaten actions they cannot legally take.

Third-Party Privacy

Collectors generally cannot discuss your debt with anyone other than you, your spouse, your attorney, or a co-signer. They can only contact others to find your location.

No Unfair Practices

Collectors cannot collect more than what you owe, deposit post-dated checks early, take your property without legal authority, or contact you by postcard.

Right to Sue

If a collector violates the FDCPA, you can sue them within one year. You may recover actual damages, statutory damages up to $1,000, and attorney's fees.

This is a summary of your rights under the FDCPA and is not legal advice. For the complete text of the law, visit the FTC website or consult with a qualified attorney. If you believe your rights have been violated, contact our team for a free case evaluation.

Protecting Yourself

How to Deal With Debt Collectors

Knowledge is power. Understanding how to communicate with debt collectors and document interactions can protect your rights and strengthen your position.

Document Everything

1

Keep a Call Log

Record the date, time, collector's name, company, phone number, and what was discussed for every call you receive.

2

Save All Written Communication

Keep every letter, email, and text message. Store originals in a safe place and make copies for your records.

3

Send Letters via Certified Mail

When sending dispute letters or cease communication requests, use certified mail with return receipt to prove delivery.

4

Request Debt Validation

Within 30 days of first contact, send a written request asking the collector to verify the debt amount and prove you owe it.

Protect Your Rights

1

Never Admit the Debt is Yours

Don't confirm personal information or acknowledge the debt until you've received written validation proving it's legitimate.

2

Know Your State's Statute of Limitations

Debt has an expiration date for legal collection. Making a payment can restart the clock, so know your state's limits before paying.

3

Don't Make Promises You Can't Keep

Never agree to a payment plan you can't afford. Verbal agreements may be binding, and missed payments can make things worse.

4

Get Everything in Writing

If you negotiate a settlement, get the terms in writing before making any payment. Include that the debt will be marked "paid in full."

Other Laws That Protect You

FCRA

Fair Credit Reporting Act

Governs how credit bureaus collect and report your information. You can dispute inaccurate items and must be notified when negative information is reported.

TCPA

Telephone Consumer Protection Act

Restricts automated calls and texts. Collectors need your consent to use autodialers or prerecorded messages to your cell phone.

State Laws

Your State's Consumer Protection Laws

Many states have additional protections beyond federal law, including stricter rules on collection practices and longer statutes of limitations for violations.
